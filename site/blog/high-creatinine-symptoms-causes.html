High Creatinine: Symptoms, Causes & When to Worry

Tiredness, swelling and foamy urine — understand what a raised creatinine level really means and the safe next steps.

RIIMS Nephrology Team Jun 2026 · 6 min read

Medically reviewed by the RIIMS nephrology team · Last reviewed: Jun 2026

Tiredness, swelling and foamy urine — understand what a raised creatinine level really means and the safe next steps.

This guide is written for patients and families in plain language. It explains the essentials, what to watch for, and how RIIMS supports you with ethical, report-based, doctor-led care — always alongside, never instead of, your treating doctor.

High creatinine often signals that the kidneys are under strain. It is a sign to investigate calmly — not a diagnosis on its own. Many people see a slightly raised number on a report and immediately fear the worst: "Is my kidney damaged? Will I need dialysis?" Before jumping to conclusions, it helps to understand what creatinine actually is, why it rises, and how doctors read it as part of a bigger picture.

What Creatinine Actually Is

Creatinine is a natural waste product made by your body. Every time your muscles do work and use energy, a small amount of creatinine is produced. Healthy kidneys filter this creatinine out of the blood and remove it through urine.

The single most important thing to understand is this: creatinine is not a disease. It is a marker — a signal. It helps doctors understand how well the kidneys are filtering, but the number alone does not tell the full story of your kidney health.

When kidney filtering capacity begins to drop, creatinine starts to build up in the blood. That is why a raised value can be an early hint that the kidneys need a closer look.

Normal Creatinine Ranges

Normal ranges can vary slightly between laboratories, but broadly:

  • Men: 0.7 to 1.3 mg/dL
  • Women: 0.6 to 1.1 mg/dL

Women often have slightly lower values than men because of differences in muscle mass. Your age, body build, and other factors can also shift the number, so a result should always be read against your own history rather than a single fixed cut-off.

Why Creatinine Rises

A higher-than-normal creatinine can have several causes, and not all of them mean long-term kidney disease. Common reasons include:

  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) — a gradual, long-standing reduction in kidney function
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — a sudden drop in kidney function, often reversible if treated early
  • Dehydration — not enough water in the body can temporarily raise the number
  • Severe infection — which can stress the kidneys
  • Certain medicines — including some painkillers and other drugs taken without guidance
  • Urinary obstruction — a blockage such as stones affecting urine flow
  • Muscular build — people with high muscle mass may naturally have higher creatinine without any kidney problem

Because the reasons are so varied, two people can have the exact same creatinine value while their actual kidney function is quite different. This is exactly why the number should never be judged in isolation.

Why Creatinine Must Be Read With Other Reports

Modern kidney guidelines around the world do not rely on creatinine alone. To understand the real state of the kidneys, doctors look at creatinine together with:

  • eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate) — this estimates how much blood your kidneys filter per minute and reflects your real filtering capacity. An eGFR of 90 or above is generally normal, 60 to 89 may be a mild reduction, and below 60 can signal a meaningful drop that needs attention.
  • Urine protein or albumin — leakage of protein into urine is an important sign of kidney stress
  • Blood pressure, blood sugar, potassium, and hemoglobin — which together reveal how the kidneys and body are coping

Think of creatinine as a warning light on a dashboard and eGFR as the actual measure of engine performance. You need to read both, along with the rest of the report, to see the true picture.

Symptoms to Be Aware Of

In its early stages, raised creatinine often causes no obvious symptoms at all — which is why routine testing matters. As kidney strain increases, some people may notice:

  • Swelling in the feet, ankles, or around the eyes
  • Unusual tiredness or weakness
  • Changes in the amount or appearance of urine
  • Poor appetite or difficulty sleeping
  • Breathlessness on exertion

These signs are not proof of serious disease on their own, but they are worth reporting to a doctor.

When to Worry: Red Flags

Not every raised report is an emergency, and the goal here is not to frighten you but to help you act at the right time. Seek prompt medical attention if you notice any of the following:

  • A sudden, sharp rise in creatinine rather than a slow change
  • A large drop in urine output, or urine almost stopping
  • Serious difficulty breathing, or heaviness in the chest
  • Continuous vomiting with nothing staying down
  • Sudden confusion, extreme drowsiness, or fainting
  • Blood in the urine or rapidly increasing swelling

Symptoms like these can point to a serious shift in kidney or electrolyte balance and should not be taken lightly.

Consult Early, Stay Calm

A high creatinine reading is a reason to investigate, not to panic. Often the smartest response is simple: get the full report reviewed by a qualified doctor, repeat the test if advised, and look at the trend over time rather than one isolated number. Catching a problem early gives you the best chance to protect your kidneys and slow any decline.

If your creatinine is raised, talk to a kidney specialist rather than searching for quick answers online. Understanding your report is the first step toward looking after your kidneys well.
