Swelling in Feet & Face: Could It Be Your Kidneys?

When edema points to the kidneys, what tests help, and when swelling means you should consult a kidney doctor.

RIIMS Nephrology Team Apr 2026 · 4 min read

Medically reviewed by the RIIMS nephrology team · Last reviewed: June 2026

When edema points to the kidneys, what tests help, and when swelling means you should consult a kidney doctor.

Swelling in the feet, ankles, or around the eyes is one of the most common reasons people first suspect something is wrong with their body. It is also one of the most misunderstood. Swelling — doctors call it oedema — simply means fluid has collected in the body's tissues. That fluid can build up for many reasons: standing all day, hot weather, a vein problem in the legs, a heart condition, a side effect of a new medicine, low protein, or a thyroid issue. The kidneys are one important cause among these — and because kidney disease is often silent in its early stages, swelling deserves to be checked properly rather than brushed aside.

This article explains how kidneys can cause swelling, how to tell different patterns apart, which reports help, and the warning signs that mean you should not wait.

Why kidneys cause swelling

Healthy kidneys do two jobs that keep fluid balanced in the body. When either job is disturbed, fluid can leak into the tissues and show up as swelling.

1. They clear extra salt and water. The kidneys act as the body's filters, removing surplus sodium and water through urine. When their function drops, salt and water are held back instead of being passed out. This excess fluid settles in the lowest, most gravity-dependent parts of the body.

2. They keep protein in the blood. In a healthy kidney, the tiny filters (nephrons) are fine enough to hold important proteins like albumin inside the bloodstream. When these filters are damaged, protein slowly leaks into the urine. As blood protein falls, the blood loses some of its ability to hold fluid inside the vessels, and fluid seeps into the tissues.

The timing and location of swelling can offer a clue:

  • Morning puffiness around the eyes often points toward a protein-related or kidney cause, because fluid has spread evenly overnight while lying flat.
  • Evening swelling of the feet and ankles is very common and has many possible causes — gravity pulls fluid downward through the day.

These are clues, not a diagnosis. Only proper tests can confirm what is actually going on.

It's not always the kidneys

Swelling is a shared symptom of several unrelated conditions. Matching the pattern to what to check helps you and your doctor look in the right direction. Use this as a guide for conversation with a doctor — not as a way to self-diagnose.

What reports to bring

If you see a doctor for swelling, a few basic reports make the visit far more useful. Carrying them saves time and helps the doctor see the full picture.

  • Serum creatinine — a blood test that reflects kidney function.
  • eGFR — an estimate of how well the kidneys are filtering, calculated from creatinine.
  • Urine routine and ACR (albumin-to-creatinine ratio) — checks whether protein is leaking into the urine, often the earliest sign of kidney stress. Modern kidney guidelines read eGFR and albuminuria together to judge risk.
  • Blood pressure record — a few readings taken over several days, since high blood pressure both causes and results from kidney and heart problems.

Depending on what the doctor finds, they may also advise a liver function test (LFT), a thyroid test, or a heart evaluation, because low protein, thyroid imbalance, and heart conditions can each cause swelling too. A kidney ultrasound may be suggested to look at kidney size and structure — but remember, an ultrasound is only one part of the picture and is read alongside creatinine and eGFR, never on its own.

Simple supportive measures

The steps below are supportive — they may help you feel more comfortable while the cause is being found and treated. They are not a treatment for kidney disease and do not replace medical care.

  • Control salt. Excess salt makes the body hold on to water. Cutting back on added salt, pickles, papad, and salty packaged snacks can ease fluid buildup.
  • Take fluids as your doctor advises. For some people, fluids need to be limited; for others, not. This is individual — follow your doctor's specific advice rather than a general rule.
  • Elevate your feet. Resting with your legs raised for a while helps gravity drain fluid from the ankles.
  • Take a light walk. Gentle movement helps circulation; avoid sitting still in one position for very long stretches.
  • Use compression stockings only if advised. These can help some people but are not right for everyone, so wear them only on a doctor's recommendation.

Red flags — see a doctor promptly

Do not treat swelling lightly. Some signs mean you should be seen quickly rather than waiting to see if it settles on its own. Seek prompt medical care if swelling comes with any of these:

  • Rapidly increasing swelling over hours or a day or two.
  • Breathlessness or difficulty breathing, especially when lying down.
  • Reduced urine output — passing much less urine than usual.
  • Chest heaviness or tightness.

These can signal that fluid is overloading the body or that the heart or kidneys need urgent attention. When in doubt, it is always safer to get checked.

Frequently asked questions

Is foot swelling always a kidney problem?

No. Swelling in the feet has many possible causes — standing for long hours, hot weather, vein problems in the legs, heart conditions, low blood protein, thyroid issues, or a side effect of certain medicines. The kidneys are one important cause to check, but not the only one. Because early kidney disease is often silent, it is worth ruling out with simple tests rather than assuming the cause. A doctor can help find out what is actually behind your swelling.

Why do my eyes puff up in the morning?

Puffiness around the eyes on waking can happen because fluid spreads evenly through the body overnight while lying flat, and the loose skin around the eyes shows it easily. In some people this pattern points toward a protein-related or kidney cause, so it is worth mentioning to a doctor and checking a urine test for protein. It is a clue, not a diagnosis — only proper tests can confirm the reason.

Which tests are done for swelling?

Commonly a doctor will start with serum creatinine and eGFR (to check kidney function), a urine routine with ACR or albumin-to-creatinine ratio (to check for protein leaking into the urine), and a blood pressure record. Depending on the findings, a liver function test, thyroid test, kidney ultrasound, or heart evaluation may also be advised, since each of these organs can contribute to swelling. Carrying any existing reports to your appointment is helpful.

When is swelling an emergency?

Swelling needs prompt medical attention if it is increasing rapidly, or if it comes with breathlessness, difficulty breathing when lying down, chest heaviness, or a clear drop in how much urine you are passing. These signs can mean fluid is overloading the body or that the heart or kidneys need urgent care. In these situations, do not wait to see if it settles — get checked without delay.

Does salt cause swelling?

Excess salt can worsen swelling because sodium makes the body hold on to water. Cutting back on added salt, pickles, papad, and salty packaged snacks is a helpful supportive step for many people with fluid retention. However, salt is only one factor — swelling still needs proper evaluation to find the underlying cause. Reducing salt supports comfort but is not a treatment for kidney or heart disease on its own.
